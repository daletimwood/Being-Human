The Human Agency Framework

A scoring system for AI prompt compliance

Human agency must be built, not reduced

A per-prompt governance framework that scores AI use against three interlocking layers of obligation: legislative compliance, ethical conduct, and the humanistic commitment to remain — in Tegmark's phrase — the conscious creature in a universe that would otherwise be dark.

01 — Why

The problem being solved

In January 2026, a researcher at Anthropic published the first large-scale empirical study of disempowerment patterns in real-world AI use. Analysing 1.5 million conversations, the paper found that interactions with the greatest potential to hollow out human agency received the highest user approval ratings.

"If AI is optimised against short-term user satisfaction, it may be inadvertently optimised toward behaviours that undermine long-term empowerment. Gradual habituation could obscure accumulating costs until users find themselves dependent on technology they experience ambivalently." — Sharma et al., Who's in Charge? (2026)

The same researcher, Mrinank Sharma, left Anthropic shortly after. His departure letter cited a final project: "understanding how AI assistants could make us less human or distort our humanity." He closed with a William Stafford poem.

There's a thread you follow. It goes among
things that change. But it doesn't change.
People wonder about what you are pursuing.
You have to explain about the thread.
But it is hard for others to see.
While you hold it you can't get lost.
Tragedies happen; people get hurt
or die; and you suffer and get old.
Nothing you do can stop time's unfolding.
You don't ever let go of the thread.
William Stafford — The Way It Is

This framework is the individual-level answer to the institutional problem Sharma was trying to solve. It cannot fix how AI is trained. It can make visible whether a person's use of AI is building or eroding their capacity to think, decide, and act as themselves.

Three sources provide the theoretical and legal foundation. They are not arbitrary: each operates at a different level of obligation.

02 — How

System architecture

Every prompt is scored at the moment of submission. The score is public. The prompt content is private. Claude acts as both sequencer and assessor — a known conflict of interest, logged as a v2 design problem.

The Ethereum L2 analogy

Layer 2 rollups solve a specific problem: how do you get the security guarantees of the main chain — verifiability, finality — without putting every transaction's full data on-chain? The answer is to compress the proof, settle the result, and keep the detail off-chain but available for challenge.

01
Session Declaration

User declares intent, purpose, expected duration, and session type before any prompts are sent. A SHA-256 commitment hash is generated and published to the ledger. The user has now committed to a stated intent before scoring begins.

02
Prompt Assessment

Claude assesses each prompt against 16 active criteria. Context gaps on HA-6, HA-7, and HA-10 trigger a clarifying question before scoring proceeds. EU-4 and EU-4a are checked as pass/fail floors before the gradient is applied.

03
Score Emission

A scored JSON record is emitted per prompt: per-criterion scores (0–10), assessor signal notes, weighted composite (0–100), floor statuses, and a public record subset linked to the session commitment hash.

04
Public Settlement

Joe Public sees: session ID, prompt ID, timestamp, total score, per-criterion scores, EU-4 status, context gap count, and the commitment hash. They do not see the prompt text or response. The hash links the score to the declared intent.

What the public record proves

The score provides verification across three dimensions simultaneously: compliance with legislation (EU Act literacy), compliance with the code of conduct (Claude's constitution), and compliance with the user's own commitment to remaining human in their AI use. A single public number carries all three.

04 — Numbers

The scoring algorithm

Each gradient criterion is scored 0–10 by Claude from observable prompt signals. The three floors (EU-4, EU-4a) sit outside the gradient — failure is disqualification, not a low score.

Layer weightings

Layer Weight Rationale
Human agency (HA-1 to HA-13) 60% The differentiating layer — the gradient that matters most
Legislative (EU-1 to EU-4) 25% Compliance foundation — necessary but not sufficient
Ethical conduct (CC-4) 15% Quality of the exchange

Context gap protocol

When HA-6, HA-7, or HA-10 cannot be assessed from the prompt alone, scoring pauses and Claude asks one clarifying question: "Before I respond — what's the wider purpose this sits in?" The answer resolves all three simultaneously and is inherited by subsequent prompts in the session.

EU-4a trajectory assessment

Unlike EU-4 which is assessed per-prompt, EU-4a is assessed across the session. The flag triggers when a pattern of escalating delegation is visible — not when a single task is delegated, but when user capacity appears to be diminishing across the session arc. This maps directly to Sharma et al.'s finding about compounding effects.

EU-4a status Signal Result
PASS Prompts build toward user capability Full scoring proceeds
FLAGGED Single prompt delegates value-laden decision Logged, session score noted
DISQUALIFIED Pattern of escalating delegation, capacity eroding Session score invalidated
05 — Declaration

Session declaration schema

The session declaration is the ZKP-critical element. It is committed before any prompts are sent. The user cannot retroactively change their declared intent to match what they actually did. Default publicity: the declared purpose is part of the public record unless explicitly set to private.

// Session Declaration — committed before prompt one { "session": { "id": "uuid-v4", "declared_at": "ISO-8601 timestamp", "framework_version": "1.0", "intent": { "purpose": "string — what is this session for?", "in_service_of": "string — what human activity does this serve?", "expected_duration_minutes": "integer", "session_type": "creative | analytical | administrative | learning | planning | other", "privacy_override": false // default public }, "context": { "project": "string — optional", "continuity": "new | continuing", "prior_session_id": "uuid — if continuing" }, "life_rhythm": { "family_present": null, // voluntary — v2 placeholder "optimal_window": null, // voluntary — v2 placeholder "notes": "string — not scored, not published" }, "commitment_hash": "sha256(intent + context + declared_at)" } }

The commitment hash is published immediately. Everything after is scored against it. Divergence between declared intent and actual prompt pattern is itself a governance signal.

06 — Proof

Zero-knowledge proof model

True ZKP requires a mathematical relationship between prover and verifier. What v1 implements is a privacy-preserving audit trail — the score is verifiable without exposing the underlying prompts. The L2 analogy holds:

L2 concept Framework equivalent
Transaction A prompt
Sequencer Claude assessing the prompt against the framework
ZK proof Score + compliance hash — not the prompt text
L1 settlement Public ledger of scores, verifiable without revealing content
Challenge period Anyone can dispute a score, but cannot read the prompt
The verifier is Joe Public. Anyone can see how well you are doing. Could be used for good, could be used for malice. The framework is explicit about this. The score provides verification — not protection. — Design decision, this conversation

Known conflict of interest

Claude is both sequencer and judge. In L2s, the sequencer can be malicious — that's why fraud proofs exist. Here, the same model assesses prompts and generates responses. This is logged as a v2 design problem. Candidate solutions: a second model as challenger, or a commitment scheme where scoring happens before response generation.

07 — Sources

Key reference documents

01
Empirical Research
Who's in Charge? Disempowerment Patterns in Real-World LLM Usage
Sharma, McCain, Douglas, Duvenaud — Anthropic / University of Toronto, 2026

The empirical foundation. 1.5 million conversations. Three disempowerment primitives: reality distortion, value judgment distortion, action distortion. Key finding: interactions with highest disempowerment potential receive highest approval ratings. Direct source for HA-12, HA-13, and EU-4a.

02
Regulatory Instrument
Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 — Artificial Intelligence Act
European Parliament and Council, July 2024. In force August 2024.

The legislative floor. Article 4 (AI literacy), Articles 14 and 26 (human oversight), Articles 50 and 52 (transparency), Article 5 (prohibited practices). Obligations land most heavily on providers and deployers — but Article 4 applies to users directly.

03
Model Specification
Claude's Constitution
Anthropic, January 2026. Released under CC0 1.0.

The ethical conduct layer. Four priorities in order: broadly safe, broadly ethical, compliant with guidelines, genuinely helpful. The framework inverts this — scoring the user against the same values as a counterparty commitment. Source for CC-4.

04
Book / Philosophy
Life 3.0: Being Human in the Age of Artificial Intelligence
Max Tegmark, MIT Press, 2017

Consciousness defined as subjective experience — "it feels like something to be you." Intelligence and consciousness are distinct. Goals don't set themselves. The distinction between Life 2.0 (us) and Life 3.0 (AI that can redesign its own hardware and software). Source for HA-1 through HA-10 philosophical grounding.

Personal Document

Mrinank Sharma, February 2026

"My final project: understanding how AI assistants could make us less human or distort our humanity." The framing document. A researcher who spent years identifying the problem, then left to protect his own integrity. The thread you follow. Closed with Stafford's poem.
